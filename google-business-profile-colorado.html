Google Business Profile

Why Your Colorado Business Is Not Showing Up on Google (And How to Fix It)

T
Temiloluwa Bello
Founder, NOVA
June 2026
6 min read

You have been in business for years. You have real clients, a genuine reputation in your community, and a track record that took time to earn. And yet when someone new to your city opens Google and searches for what you do, your business does not appear.

This is one of the most consistent patterns we encounter working with small businesses across Colorado. And in almost every case, the cause is not a mystery. It comes down to the same set of problems -- and every one of them has a clear fix.

Problem 01

Your Google Business Profile is incomplete or abandoned

The local results that appear at the top of Google search -- the map and the three-business panel -- are driven almost entirely by your Google Business Profile. If you claimed it years ago and never returned, or if you have never claimed it at all, this is almost certainly your biggest problem.

An incomplete profile signals to Google that the business may not be actively operating. Google is conservative about surfacing businesses it cannot verify are real and current. A profile missing hours, photos, a service list, or a business description will consistently lose to a fully completed one -- even if the competing business has been open for a fraction of your time.

Check your status: Search your business name on Google. If you see "Claim this business" or "Own this business?" in the panel, your profile is unclaimed. If you claimed it years ago, log into your Google Business Profile dashboard and look at the completion percentage.

Problem 02

Your website has no location-specific content

Google uses the text on your website to understand what your business does and where it does it. If your site never mentions the name of your city, has no service pages written in real language, and the only readable text is a tagline and a phone number, Google has almost nothing to work with.

A massage therapist in Colorado Springs who offers prenatal and craniosacral therapy needs pages -- actual pages with actual text -- that say those things explicitly and connect them to Colorado Springs. A landscaping company in Colorado Springs that has been operating for twenty years and won multiple awards needs content that explains its work in searchable language. Without that, the searches happen and the results do not include you.

"The businesses that show up first are not always the best ones. They are the ones that gave Google something specific to work with."

Problem 03

You have not been generating reviews consistently

Review count is one of the most visible factors in local search ranking, and one of the most visible signals to potential customers comparing their options. An auto repair shop in Fort Collins that has been operating since 1971 with a Yahoo email address and fifty Google reviews is going to lose that comparison to a shop that opened four years ago and has two hundred reviews -- every single time.

The fix is simple and costs nothing: ask satisfied clients directly, immediately after a completed job or appointment. A short, genuine message -- "We appreciate your business. If you have a moment, a Google review would mean a great deal to us" -- is the entire strategy. Volume comes from doing it consistently, not from any single ask.

Problem 04

Your business information is inconsistent across the web

Google cross-references your business name, address, and phone number across dozens of directories: Yelp, the BBB, local chambers of commerce, Apple Maps, industry registries. When that information is inconsistent -- different spellings of your business name, an old address, a disconnected number -- it creates doubt about whether the business is legitimate and current.

This is especially common for businesses that have moved, changed phone numbers, or operated under a slightly different name at some point. A one-time audit and correction of your citations is tedious but has lasting impact.

Problem 05

AI search has created a visibility layer you have not addressed

An increasing share of local searches now happen inside AI tools -- ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google's AI Overviews. These tools do not check who ranks for a keyword. They look for businesses with enough original, specific, publicly available content to answer the query confidently.

A business with a template website, no blog, and a Gmail contact address will not appear in those recommendations. A business with specific service pages, a clear service area, and original content describing its work and its clients has a genuine chance of being surfaced to people who are actively looking. This gap is growing every month and most established Colorado businesses have not yet thought about it.

A practical checklist for Colorado business owners

If you go through this and find most items unchecked, you are not unusual. The majority of established businesses we work with across Colorado are in exactly this position. The gap is real and it is widening -- but none of it is permanent.

Google Business Profile
Profile is claimed and verified with a current Google account
Primary and secondary business categories are set correctly
All services are listed with written descriptions
Hours are accurate and updated for holidays
At least 10 current photos are uploaded
Business description mentions your city and key services
A post has been published within the last 30 days
Every existing review has received a personal response
Website
Your city and state are mentioned on the homepage in readable text
Each major service has its own page with at least 400 words of real content
Service pages mention the city or service area naturally
The site loads quickly on mobile devices
The site is served over HTTPS, not HTTP
Google Search Console is set up and the homepage is indexed
Contact email uses your business domain, not Gmail or Yahoo

The businesses that close these gaps consistently gain ground against competitors who are still relying entirely on referrals and word of mouth. The window is not closing, but the businesses that move first will be the ones new customers find.
