Why Your Colorado Business Is Not Showing Up on Google (And How to Fix It)

T
Temiloluwa Bello
Founder, NOVA
June 2026
6 min read
Try this before you read further

Search for your business on Google the way a new customer would -- not by name, but by what you do and where you are. "Plumber Montrose Colorado." "Massage therapist Colorado Springs." "Dog groomer Loveland."

Does your business appear? Where? What does your listing look like compared to the three results above you? Most business owners have never done this. The ones who have are usually surprised by what they find -- and not in a good way.

Years in business. A real community reputation. Clients who have referred you for years. And yet when someone new to your city searches for what you do, your business does not appear. This is one of the most common and most fixable situations we encounter working with small businesses across Colorado.

In almost every case, the cause is not a single problem. It is a cluster of the same problems -- the same five, appearing in the same order, across businesses in every industry. Here is what they are and how to fix each one.

The five reasons

Problem 01

Your Google Business Profile is incomplete or abandoned

The local results panel at the top of Google search -- the map and the three business listings -- is driven almost entirely by your Google Business Profile. If you claimed it years ago and never returned, or if you have never claimed it at all, this is almost certainly your most significant problem.

An incomplete profile tells Google that your business may not be actively operating. Google is conservative by design -- it would rather not show a business than show one that is closed or has incorrect information. A profile missing hours, photos, a service list, or a business description will consistently lose to a fully built one, even if the competing business has been open for a fraction of your time.

The fix

Search your business name on Google. If you see "Claim this business" in the panel, the profile is unclaimed. If you claimed it years ago, log into your Google Business Profile dashboard at business.google.com and work through every empty field. Categories, services, hours, photos, description. Do not leave any of them blank.

Problem 02

Your website has no location-specific content

Google uses the text on your website to understand what your business does and where it serves customers. If your site never mentions the name of your city, has no service pages written in real language, and the only readable text is a tagline and a phone number, Google has almost nothing to work with when deciding whether to surface you for a local search.

The gap is often severe. A massage therapist in Colorado Springs offering prenatal and craniosacral therapy needs pages that say those words explicitly and connect them to Colorado Springs. A landscaping company in Colorado Springs with twenty years of work and multiple awards needs content that explains its services in the language searchers actually use. Without that, the searches happen and the results do not include you.

The fix

Identify the three to five most common searches your ideal new client would run. Create a dedicated page for each one -- with real written content, not just a title. Name the service specifically. Name the city. Write at least 400 words of genuinely useful information that a person would want to read. That page, published on its own URL, is what Google can index and rank.

Problem 03

You are not generating reviews consistently

Review count is one of the most visible ranking factors in local search, and one of the first things a potential customer notices when comparing their options. An auto repair shop in Fort Collins that has been operating since 1971 with fifty Google reviews is going to lose the first-glance comparison to a shop that opened four years ago and has two hundred reviews -- every time, regardless of actual quality.

Most businesses with low review counts are not doing anything wrong. They simply have never built asking for reviews into their process. Satisfied customers rarely leave reviews spontaneously. They do when asked directly, warmly, and immediately after a positive interaction.

The fix

Build a one-sentence ask into every completed job or appointment: "If you have a moment, a Google review would mean a great deal to our small business." Send your Google review link directly. Do not expect customers to find it themselves. Volume comes from consistency, not from any individual ask.

Problem 04

Your business information is inconsistent across the web

Google cross-references your business name, address, and phone number across dozens of external sources: Yelp, the Better Business Bureau, local chamber of commerce directories, Apple Maps, industry registries. When that information is inconsistent -- different spellings of your business name, an old address, a phone number that was disconnected two years ago -- Google's confidence in your listing drops, and so does your ranking.

This problem is especially common for businesses that have moved, changed phone numbers, rebranded slightly, or added a DBA at some point. You may have been in business for twenty years with the same address, but if a directory from fifteen years ago still lists the old one, it creates a conflict that quietly costs you ranking every day.

The fix

Search your business name on Google and check the top ten directory results. Look for any inconsistencies in name, address, or phone number. Correct each one at the source. This is tedious the first time and then requires almost no ongoing maintenance.

Problem 05

You have not addressed AI search at all

An increasing share of local searches now happen inside AI tools: ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google's own AI Overviews. When someone asks one of these tools to recommend a business, it does not check keyword rankings. It looks for businesses with enough original, specific, publicly available content to answer the question with confidence.

A business with a template website, a Gmail contact address, and no original written content will not appear in those answers -- not because the business is not good, but because there is nothing for the AI to find and cite. A business with specific service pages, a clear geographic footprint, and original writing about its work has a real chance of being surfaced to people who are actively looking for exactly what it offers.

The fix

The same content that helps you rank in Google search helps AI tools find and recommend you. Specific service pages, original descriptions of your work, clear location information -- all of it feeds into how AI systems understand and surface businesses. Start with the content gap, and AI visibility follows.

The businesses that show up first are not always the best ones. They are the ones that made themselves readable to machines.
Temiloluwa Bello, Founder of NOVA

A practical checklist to work through

If you go through this list and find most items unchecked, you are not unusual. The majority of established businesses we have encountered across Colorado are in exactly this position. The gap is real, it is measurable, and none of it is permanent.

Google Business Profile
Profile is claimed and verified with a current Google account
Primary and secondary business categories are correctly set
All services are listed with written descriptions
Hours are accurate and updated for holidays
At least 10 current, high-quality photos are uploaded
Business description mentions your city and key services
A post has been published in the last 30 days
Every existing review has received a personal response
Website content
Your city and state are mentioned in readable text on the homepage
Each major service has its own page with real written content
Service pages mention the city or service area naturally in the text
The site loads in under three seconds on a mobile device
The site is served over HTTPS, not HTTP
Google Search Console is set up and the site is being indexed
Contact email uses your business domain, not Gmail or Yahoo
Citations and consistency
Business name is spelled identically across all directories
Address is formatted identically everywhere it appears
Phone number is current and consistent across all listings
Yelp, BBB, Apple Maps, and chamber listings are up to date

The businesses that close these gaps consistently gain ground against competitors who are still relying entirely on referrals and reputation built before the internet became the primary way new customers make decisions. The window is not closing -- but the businesses that move first will be the ones new customers find.
